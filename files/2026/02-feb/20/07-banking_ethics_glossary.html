Terminologies, Hard Words & Their Meanings

01
Ethics
noun
A system of moral principles that govern a person's or institution's behaviour, especially regarding honesty, fairness, and responsibility.
Example
The bank was praised for upholding strong ethics even when regulators were not watching.
02
Compliance
noun
The act of conforming to rules, regulations, laws, or standards set by an authority or governing body.
Example
Strict compliance with RBI guidelines ensured the bank avoided any regulatory penalty.
03
Transparency
noun
The quality of being open, honest, and free from hidden agendas, especially in communications and financial reporting.
Example
Complete transparency in loan documentation helped customers understand every fee involved.
04
Custodian
noun
A person or institution that is responsible for safeguarding and managing something on behalf of others, such as money or assets.
Example
As a custodian of public deposits, the bank must prioritise the safety of every rupee entrusted to it.
05
Deregulated
adjective
Describes an environment in which government controls and restrictions on an industry have been reduced or removed.
Example
In a deregulated banking sector, institutions must self-regulate to prevent unethical practices.
06
Grievance Redressal
noun phrase
A formal mechanism through which complaints or concerns raised by customers or employees are addressed and resolved.
Example
The bank set up a 24-hour grievance redressal portal to handle customer disputes swiftly.
07
Coercion
noun
The practice of persuading or forcing someone to act in a desired way through threats, pressure, or intimidation.
Example
Any form of coercion to meet sales targets can erode employee morale and institutional trust.
08
Mis-selling
noun
The practice of selling a financial product or service to a customer that is inappropriate for their needs, often through misleading information.
Example
The regulatory fine was imposed after the bank was found guilty of mis-selling insurance policies to senior citizens.
09
Institutional Ethics
noun phrase
The collective moral standards, values, and principles that guide the decisions and policies of an organisation as a whole.
Example
Institutional ethics at the cooperative bank were embedded in every HR policy and lending guideline.
10
Performance-Linked Pay
noun phrase
A compensation structure where a portion of an employee's salary is tied directly to measurable performance outcomes or targets.
Example
The introduction of performance-linked pay raised productivity but also increased pressure to sell unsuitable products.
11
Supervisory Mechanism
noun phrase
A set of oversight processes, controls, and checks put in place to monitor and regulate activities within an institution.
Example
Robust supervisory mechanisms were required once AI began making autonomous lending decisions.
12
Moral Responsibility
noun phrase
The obligation to act in a manner that is ethically right, and to be accountable for the outcomes of one's actions.
Example
The chairman stressed that moral responsibility extends beyond rule-following to genuine care for depositors.
13
Digital Lending
noun phrase
The process of providing loans through online or electronic platforms, from application and assessment to disbursal, without physical interaction.
Example
Digital lending platforms have made credit accessible to millions but require careful ethical oversight in decision-making.
14
Credit Appraisal
noun phrase
The evaluation of a borrower's creditworthiness and capacity to repay a loan, typically assessing income, assets, and repayment history.
Example
Ethical credit appraisal ensures that loans are not pushed on to applicants who cannot repay them.
15
Artificial Intelligence (AI)
noun phrase
The simulation of human intelligence by computer systems, enabling machines to perform tasks like reasoning, learning, and decision-making.
Example
As artificial intelligence automates loan approvals, human oversight with ethical judgment becomes even more critical.
16
Onboarding
noun/gerund
The process of formally welcoming and integrating a new customer or employee into a service or organisation, including documentation and verification.
Example
The digital onboarding process must verify customer identity ethically, without bias or discrimination.
17
Regulatory Philosophy
noun phrase
The underlying principles and values that guide an institution's approach to adhering to regulations and dealing with regulatory bodies.
Example
A bank's regulatory philosophy should prioritise the spirit of the law rather than mere letter-compliance.
18
Reserve Bank of India (RBI)
proper noun
India's central banking institution, responsible for regulating the issue of currency, monetary policy, and the supervision of the banking sector.
Example
The Reserve Bank of India issued new draft guidelines to ensure banks market products with full honesty.
19
Deputy Governor
noun phrase
A senior official in a central bank who assists the Governor in overseeing monetary policy, regulation, and banking supervision.
Example
The Deputy Governor delivered an address warning that banks must go beyond compliance to embrace genuine ethics.
20
Draft Guidelines
noun phrase
A preliminary version of rules or instructions, released for consultation or review before being finalised and enacted officially.
Example
The draft guidelines on financial product advertising invited public comments before their final publication.
21
Autonomy
noun
The right or condition of self-governance; in banking, the freedom institutions have to set their own policies within regulatory boundaries.
Example
Greater autonomy in setting lending policies comes with the corresponding responsibility to act ethically.
22
Board-Approved Policies
noun phrase
Rules, frameworks, or procedures that have been formally reviewed and authorised by a company's board of directors.
Example
All board-approved policies on customer data must reflect the bank's commitment to privacy and transparency.
23
Underscored
verb (past tense)
Emphasised or highlighted the importance of something; drew special attention to a point.
Example
The regulator underscored that lapses in ethical conduct would be treated as seriously as financial fraud.
24
Unequivocally
adverb
In a way that leaves no doubt; absolutely clearly and without any ambiguity or uncertainty.
Example
The CEO stated unequivocally that customer interest would always take precedence over short-term profits.
25
Inward Honesty
noun phrase
Genuine, self-initiated truthfulness that originates from an institution's own values rather than being forced by external scrutiny.
Example
Inward honesty in reporting must precede any promise of transparency made to the public.
26
Sensitised
verb (past tense)
Made aware of and responsive to particular issues, emotions, or ethical concerns through training or education.
Example
Bank staff were sensitised to the special needs of elderly customers through a dedicated empathy workshop.
27
Empathy
noun
The ability to understand and share the feelings of another person, especially in customer-facing roles where individual circumstances differ.
Example
Frontline officers must demonstrate empathy when dealing with customers who have faced financial distress.
28
Frontline Staff
noun phrase
Employees who directly interact with customers at the point of service, representing the institution in face-to-face or digital transactions.
Example
Frontline staff are often the first to notice when a customer may be sold an unsuitable financial product.
29
Motivational Drives
noun phrase
Organised campaigns or programmes designed to inspire and energise employees to perform better and achieve targets.
Example
Motivational drives can boost sales numbers but must never create unethical pressure on employees.
30
Security Thread (metaphor)
noun phrase
In the article, used metaphorically to describe ethics as something invisibly woven into institutional policies, much like the thin strip embedded in currency notes to prevent forgery.
Example
Ethical values should run through every policy like a security thread, invisible in ordinary handling but vital to authenticity.
31
Procedural Conformity
noun phrase
Following established procedures and steps mechanically, without necessarily embracing the deeper intent or spirit behind those rules.
Example
Mere procedural conformity may satisfy auditors but is insufficient to build genuine public trust in a financial institution.
32
Audit Functions
noun phrase
The processes of systematic examination and verification of financial records, operations, and controls to ensure accuracy, legality, and ethical conduct.
Example
Strengthened audit functions were deployed to check whether automated reports reflected fair and accurate information.
33
Gamut
noun
The complete range or entire scope of something; used to signify the full breadth of a category of activities or elements.
Example
The new policy covered the entire gamut of banking operations, from account opening to loan closure.
34
Digitised
adjective/verb (past participle)
Converted into or performed in a digital electronic format, replacing paper-based or manual processes.
Example
Fully digitised loan applications reduced processing time but introduced new risks of algorithmic bias.
35
Alternative Channels
noun phrase
Non-branch methods of banking service delivery, such as ATMs, internet banking, mobile banking apps, and phone banking.
Example
Over 95% of banking transactions now take place via alternative channels, reducing the need for physical branch visits.
Example
Banks must apply the same ethical standards across all alternative channels as they do at the physical counter.
36
Human Interface
noun phrase
The point at which human judgment, interaction, and decision-making occur within a technological or automated process.
Example
As AI reduces human interface in banking, ethical supervision must compensate for the absence of human judgment.
37
Predominant
adjective
Present as the strongest or main element; having or exerting control or power over others.
Example
AI has become predominant in fraud detection, handling millions of transactions that humans could not review manually.
38
Corollary
noun
A natural consequence or result that follows logically from something already established or proven.
Example
A culture of transparency is the natural corollary of an institution that truly values its customers over profits.
39
Soft Theme
noun phrase (colloquial)
A subject or concern regarded as secondary, optional, or less rigorous than hard, quantifiable, or legally binding requirements.
Example
Ethics in lending should never be dismissed as a soft theme; it is as critical as capital adequacy ratios.
40
Oversight
noun
The act of supervising or watching over a process or group to ensure proper functioning, accuracy, and adherence to standards.
Example
Effective regulatory oversight prevented the bank from adopting aggressive digital marketing tactics.
41
HR Function
noun phrase
The human resources department or role within an organisation, responsible for recruiting, training, managing, and motivating staff.
Example
The HR function must ensure that performance targets do not push staff into ethically questionable sales behaviour.
42
Prescribed Timelines
noun phrase
Formally set or mandated time limits within which a task, target, or resolution must be completed.
Example
Grievances must be resolved within the prescribed timelines to maintain customer confidence in the bank's accountability.
43
Marketing
noun
The activities and strategies used to promote and sell products or services, including advertising, branding, and customer communication.
Example
Ethical marketing of financial products requires honest disclosure of all risks involved to the customer.
44
Aggressive Marketing
noun phrase
A sales approach that uses high-pressure tactics or persistent persuasion to push products onto customers, often disregarding suitability or consent.
Example
Aggressive marketing of investment products to retirees led to regulatory action and reputational damage for the lender.
45
Institutional Stability
noun phrase
The ability of an organisation to maintain consistent, reliable, and sound functioning over time, especially in the face of pressures or challenges.
Example
A positive management-employee relationship is the backbone of long-term institutional stability in any bank.
46
Internal Reporting Mechanism
noun phrase
Systems and channels within an organisation through which employees can report concerns, irregularities, or data, including whistleblowing channels.
Example
A robust internal reporting mechanism allows staff to flag ethical violations without fear of retaliation.
47
Balance Sheet
noun phrase
A financial statement that summarises an institution's assets, liabilities, and shareholders' equity at a specific point in time.
Example
Even an automated balance sheet must be reviewed by ethical supervisors to ensure nothing material has been concealed.
48
Financial Products
noun phrase
Instruments or services offered by financial institutions such as loans, insurance policies, mutual funds, deposits, and credit cards.
Example
Customers must be given clear and accurate information about all financial products before they sign any agreement.
49
Incentive Schemes
noun phrase
Structured reward programmes offered to employees to encourage higher performance, sales, or achievement of specific organisational goals.
Example
Poorly designed incentive schemes, such as luxury trips for top sellers, may incentivise unethical product pushing.
50
Permissible
adjective
Allowed or acceptable within a set of rules, laws, or norms, even if not necessarily advisable or ethical in all circumstances.
Example
While technically permissible, linking foreign trips to sales performance can quietly encourage mis-selling over time.
