7 Key Terms Explained

Terminology Glossary

1 Economics

Unemployment Rate

/ ˌʌn.ɪmˈplɔɪ.mənt reɪt /

The percentage of people in the labour force who are currently without a job but are actively seeking work. It only counts those who are available and looking — not those who have given up searching.

Example Sentence

"When the unemployment rate climbed from 4.8% to 5%, economists warned that more households would struggle to meet basic expenses."

2 Labour

Labour Force

/ ˈleɪ.bər fɔːrs /

The total number of people who are either employed or actively looking for employment. It excludes students, retirees, homemakers, and those not seeking work. Also called the workforce.

Example Sentence

"India's labour force includes hundreds of millions of workers in both the organized and unorganized sectors."

3 Statistics

Labour Force Participation Rate

/ LFPR /

The proportion of the working-age population (15 years and above) that is either employed or actively seeking employment. A lower LFPR means fewer people are even trying to enter the job market.

Example Sentence

"The drop in the labour force participation rate suggested that many discouraged workers had stopped looking for jobs altogether."

4 Statistics

Worker-Population Ratio

/ WPR /

Also called the employment-to-population ratio, it measures the share of the total working-age population that is actually employed. It is a broader and often more telling metric than the unemployment rate alone.

Example Sentence

"A declining worker-population ratio in both rural and urban areas indicated that fewer adults were gainfully employed in January."

5 Survey

Periodic Labour Force Survey

/ PLFS /

A continuous household survey conducted by India's Ministry of Statistics (MoSPI) to measure key labour indicators — including employment, unemployment, and wages — on a quarterly and annual basis since 2017.

Example Sentence

"The Periodic Labour Force Survey is India's most authoritative source for tracking employment trends across rural and urban households."

6 Economics

Marginal Increase

/ ˈmɑːr.dʒɪ.nəl ɪnˈkriːs /

A very small or slight rise in a value — minimal enough that it may not signal a major structural change. In economics, "marginal" often refers to a change at the edge or boundary of a phenomenon, rather than a dramatic shift.

Example Sentence

"Economists described the 0.2 percentage-point rise in unemployment as a marginal increase, unlikely to trigger immediate policy intervention."

7 Labour

Rural vs. Urban Areas

/ ˈrʊər.əl · ˈɜː.bən /

In the context of Indian labour surveys, rural areas refer to villages and agrarian communities, while urban areas refer to towns and cities. Employment conditions, wage levels, and job types differ significantly between these two categories.

Example Sentence

"The unemployment rate recorded a simultaneous rise in both rural and urban areas, indicating that the slowdown was not confined to any single region of India."
