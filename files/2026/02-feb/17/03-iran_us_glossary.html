01 Diplomacy
Nuclear Talks
/ ˈnjuːklɪə tɔːks /
Formal negotiations between states focused on limiting, verifying, or eliminating a country's nuclear weapons or enrichment capabilities. They typically involve multiple rounds over months or years, often mediated by a neutral third party, and cover inspections, sanctions relief, and enrichment caps.
Example sentence
The second round of nuclear talks in Geneva brought both sides back to the table, though Iran insisted it would not sign any deal under the threat of military force.
02 Diplomacy
Mediation
/ ˌmiːdiˈeɪʃən /
A conflict-resolution process in which a neutral third party (the mediator) facilitates negotiations between two or more parties who cannot, or will not, negotiate directly. The mediator has no power to impose a settlement — their role is to build trust, carry messages, and bridge gaps.
Example sentence
Oman's quiet mediation proved essential: without a trusted intermediary, Washington and Tehran would have had no back-channel through which to arrange the Geneva meeting.
03 Diplomacy
Envoy
/ ˈenvɔɪ /
A diplomat or special representative sent by a government to conduct negotiations or represent its interests on a specific issue. Unlike an ambassador (a permanent presence), an envoy is often dispatched for a defined mission and reports directly to the head of state or a senior minister.
Example sentence
Steve Witkoff, Trump's special envoy for West Asia, met Araghchi in Muscat and laid the groundwork that brought both parties to the negotiating table in Geneva.
04 Diplomacy
Submission
/ səbˈmɪʃən /
In diplomatic language, capitulating to a stronger party's demands under coercion or threat rather than through genuine negotiation. When Araghchi said "submission before threats" is "not on the table," he was signalling that Iran will not make concessions driven by fear of military action — only by genuine mutual agreement.
Example sentence
Tehran drew a sharp distinction between compromise and submission, warning that any deal signed under duress would lack legitimacy and domestic support.
05 Diplomacy
Sanctions
/ ˈsæŋkʃənz /
Economic or political penalties imposed by one country (or group of countries) on another to pressure a change in behaviour. They can target banking, oil exports, arms trade, or specific officials. In the Iran context, US sanctions have crippled the Iranian economy and are typically the central bargaining chip in nuclear negotiations.
Example sentence
Iran's chief demand in any nuclear deal has consistently been the lifting of US sanctions that have blocked its access to global financial markets and oil revenues.
06 Diplomacy
Back-channel
/ bæk ˈtʃænəl /
Secret or informal lines of communication between rival governments, operating outside official diplomatic channels. Back-channels allow adversaries to explore possibilities, test positions, and lay groundwork for agreements without public commitment or loss of face. Oman has historically hosted US–Iran back-channel communications.
Example sentence
Months of quiet back-channel exchanges in Muscat preceded the formal announcement of Geneva talks, giving both sides confidence that a second meeting was worthwhile.
07 Military
Aircraft Carrier
/ ˈeəkrɑːft ˈkærɪə /
A massive warship that serves as a floating airbase, carrying dozens of combat aircraft and acting as the centrepiece of a naval strike group. Deploying an aircraft carrier to a region is a powerful signal of military intent. The US deploying a second carrier to the Persian Gulf area was a deliberate show of force aimed at Iran.
Example sentence
The arrival of a second US aircraft carrier in the region sent an unmistakable message: Washington was prepared to back its diplomatic ultimatum with overwhelming naval firepower.
08 Military
Live-fire Drill
/ laɪv faɪər drɪl /
A military exercise in which actual weapons — ammunition, missiles, or torpedoes — are used rather than simulated ones. Live-fire drills demonstrate real combat readiness and are often timed to send a political signal to adversaries. The name "Smart Control of Hormuz Strait" made Iran's intended audience clear.
Example sentence
The IRGC's live-fire drills near the Strait of Hormuz were a calculated reminder that Iran retains the ability to disrupt one of the world's most critical shipping lanes.
09 Military
Naval Forces
/ ˈneɪvəl fɔːsɪz /
The maritime branch of a country's armed forces, responsible for warfare at sea and the projection of power along coastlines and through key waterways. The IRGC Navy (separate from Iran's conventional navy) operates fast-attack boats, submarines, and anti-ship missiles in the Gulf.
Example sentence
The IRGC's naval forces have a long record of harassing commercial tankers in the Persian Gulf, using swarms of speedboats to intimidate far larger vessels.
10 Military
Operational Readiness
/ ɒpəˈreɪʃənəl ˈredɪnəs /
The degree to which a military unit is prepared to perform its assigned missions at any given time. Factors include personnel training, equipment maintenance, fuel and ammunition supply, and intelligence. Drills are the primary method for testing and demonstrating operational readiness to both allies and adversaries.
Example sentence
The IRGC Navy used the Hormuz drills to evaluate the operational readiness of its fast-boat squadrons, mine-laying units, and shore-based anti-ship missile batteries.
11 Military
Mockup
/ ˈmɒkʌp /
A full-scale physical replica of a military target — typically an enemy vessel or installation — used in training and propaganda. Iran has famously constructed mockup US aircraft carriers that its forces "attack" during exercises, both to practise tactics and to create dramatic footage for domestic and international audiences.
Example sentence
Images of IRGC speedboats encircling an Iranian mockup of a US aircraft carrier drew immediate mockery in Washington but served a powerful psychological purpose at home.
12 Military
Chokepoint
/ ˈtʃəʊkpɔɪnt /
A narrow strategic waterway through which a large proportion of global trade or military traffic must pass, making it extremely vulnerable to blockade or attack. Control — or the credible threat of blocking — a chokepoint gives a nation disproportionate leverage in geopolitical crises.
Example sentence
As the sole maritime chokepoint between the Gulf and the open ocean, the Strait of Hormuz is Iran's most potent geographical trump card in any confrontation with the West.
13 Geography
Strait of Hormuz
/ streɪt əv hɔːˈmuːz /
A narrow waterway between Iran and Oman connecting the Persian Gulf to the Gulf of Oman and the Arabian Sea. At its narrowest it is just 33km wide. Roughly 20% of global oil trade passes through it daily — making it the world's most strategically vital maritime chokepoint. Iran has repeatedly threatened to close it during crises.
Example sentence
A blockade of the Strait of Hormuz would trigger an immediate global oil price shock, cutting off supplies to Japan, South Korea, India, and much of Europe within days.
14 Geography
Persian Gulf
/ ˈpɜːʒən ɡʌlf /
A semi-enclosed sea bordered by Iran, Iraq, Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, Bahrain, Qatar, the UAE, and Oman. It is the world's most oil-rich body of water, with the surrounding nations holding the majority of global proven oil reserves. Its geopolitical importance makes it one of the most militarised maritime regions on earth.
Example sentence
The US Fifth Fleet, permanently based in Bahrain, patrols the Persian Gulf to protect freedom of navigation for the oil tankers that are the lifeblood of the global economy.
15 Geography
Arabian Sea
/ əˈreɪbɪən siː /
The northwestern part of the Indian Ocean, bounded by the Arabian Peninsula, the Indian subcontinent, and the Horn of Africa. It is the open ocean that vessels reach after exiting the Strait of Hormuz and the Gulf of Oman. Major shipping lanes to Europe, Asia, and Africa pass through it.
Example sentence
US naval assets positioned in the Arabian Sea can surge into the Gulf of Oman within hours if the Strait of Hormuz is threatened, maintaining a forward deterrent posture.
16 Geography
Geneva
/ dʒɪˈniːvə /
Switzerland's second-largest city and the world's foremost centre of international diplomacy. Home to the UN's European headquarters, the ICRC, the WTO, and countless multilateral treaty negotiations. Its neutral status under Swiss law makes it the preferred venue for sensitive talks between adversarial states, including past Iran nuclear negotiations.
Example sentence
Choosing Geneva as the venue was itself a signal: Switzerland's strict neutrality guaranteed both sides a setting where neither could claim home-team advantage.
17 Geography
Muscat
/ ˈmʌskæt /
The capital city of Oman, strategically located on the Gulf of Oman. Muscat has carved out a unique role as a neutral diplomatic hub — Oman maintains friendly relations with both the United States and Iran, making it the preferred venue for sensitive US–Iran back-channel communications since at least the 1980s.
Example sentence
The February meeting in Muscat was the first direct US–Iran engagement of the Trump second term, and its positive conclusion paved the way for the higher-profile Geneva summit.
18 Nuclear
Nuclear Enrichment
/ ˈnjuːklɪər ɪnˈrɪtʃmənt /
The process of increasing the concentration of the fissile isotope uranium-235 in uranium ore. Low enrichment (3–5%) produces fuel for civilian nuclear power plants. Higher enrichment (90%+) produces weapons-grade material. Iran's advancing enrichment levels — crossing 60% and approaching 90% — are the core concern driving international pressure.
Example sentence
The central sticking point in negotiations is how far Iran will roll back its nuclear enrichment programme — and what economic incentives the West will offer in return.
19 Nuclear
Nuclear Facility
/ ˈnjuːklɪər fəˈsɪlɪti /
A site involved in nuclear research, uranium enrichment, fuel fabrication, or weapons development. Key Iranian facilities include Natanz (main enrichment site), Fordow (buried underground), and Arak (heavy-water reactor). These sites were targeted in the June 2025 Israeli-US strikes, prompting Iran to expel IAEA inspectors.
Example sentence
Iran suspended IAEA access to its key nuclear facilities after the June 2025 strikes, leaving the international community blind to the true state of its atomic programme.
20 Nuclear
Inspections / Access
/ ɪnˈspekʃənz /
Formal visits by IAEA inspectors to a country's nuclear sites to verify that declared activities match actual activities, and to detect any covert weapons development. Inspections are the backbone of the global nuclear non-proliferation regime. Iran's suspension of access after the strikes was a major escalatory step.
Example sentence
Restoring IAEA inspections access to Iranian enrichment sites is likely a prerequisite for any US sanctions relief — but Tehran insists security guarantees must come first.
21 Nuclear
Proliferation
/ prəˌlɪfəˈreɪʃən /
The spread of nuclear weapons, materials, or technology to states or non-state actors that do not currently possess them. Nuclear non-proliferation is a cornerstone of global security policy, enshrined in the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT). Iran's programme is widely viewed as the most serious near-term proliferation risk.
Example sentence
US officials warned that without a new deal, Iran's breakout timeline — the time needed to build a bomb — could shorten to weeks, triggering a regional proliferation cascade as Saudi Arabia and others sought their own deterrents.
22 Organisation
IRGC (Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps)
/ aɪ.ɑː.dʒiː.siː /
Iran's elite parallel military force, founded in 1979 to protect the Islamic Revolution. Separate from the conventional army, the IRGC has its own ground, naval, and air forces, plus the Quds Force for extraterritorial operations. It controls large segments of Iran's economy and is designated a terrorist organisation by the United States.
Example sentence
The IRGC's decision to launch naval drills while its foreign minister negotiated in Geneva was a deliberate dual-track strategy — diplomacy and military posturing conducted simultaneously.
23 Organisation
IAEA (International Atomic Energy Agency)
/ aɪ.eɪ.iː.ˈeɪ /
A UN-affiliated intergovernmental organisation headquartered in Vienna that promotes the peaceful use of nuclear energy while working to prevent nuclear weapons proliferation. Its inspectors monitor nuclear sites worldwide and report violations to the UN Security Council. It is the world's nuclear watchdog.
Example sentence
IAEA chief Rafael Grossi's "in-depth technical discussions" with Araghchi were seen as a potential first step toward restoring inspector access to Iranian nuclear sites shuttered since June 2025.
24 Organisation
Press TV
/ pres tiː viː /
Iran's English-language state-run international news network, launched in 2007. Funded and operated by the Islamic Republic of Iran Broadcasting (IRIB), it broadcasts Iran's official government narrative globally. Its reporting reflects Iranian government positions and is considered a state propaganda outlet by Western governments, which have banned it from several satellite platforms.
Example sentence
Press TV framed the Hormuz drills as defensive preparations against foreign aggression, presenting the same exercises that Western media described as provocative saber-rattling.
25 Key Person
Abbas Araghchi
/ ˈæbəs ˌærəɡˈtʃiː /
Iran's Foreign Minister, appointed in 2024. A veteran diplomat with deep experience in nuclear negotiations — he was a key figure in the 2015 JCPOA talks. Known for combining firm adherence to Iran's red lines with a pragmatic willingness to engage. His Geneva appearance signals Tehran's genuine, if conditional, interest in a deal.
Example sentence
Araghchi's social media post from Geneva — "real ideas, but not submission before threats" — was carefully worded to reassure Iran's hardliners while signalling flexibility to his Western interlocutors.
26 Key Person
Steve Witkoff
/ stiːv ˈwɪtkɒf /
US President Donald Trump's special envoy for West Asia (Middle East) in his second term. A real estate businessman turned diplomat, Witkoff was Trump's choice for sensitive regional negotiations including the Gaza ceasefire and Iran nuclear talks. He led the US delegation at the February 2025 Muscat talks that set up the Geneva meeting.
Example sentence
Steve Witkoff's unorthodox diplomatic style — blunt and transactional — was seen by some analysts as a feature, not a bug, when dealing with Iran's equally hard-nosed negotiators.
27 Key Person
Rafael Grossi
/ ˌræfɪˈel ˈɡrɒsi /
The Director General of the IAEA since 2019. An Argentine diplomat and nuclear expert who has persistently pressed Iran to restore transparency and inspector access to its nuclear sites. His public warnings about Iran's advancing programme have kept international pressure focused on Tehran's nuclear activities throughout the crisis period.
Example sentence
Rafael Grossi's statement that he had held "in-depth technical discussions" with Araghchi was interpreted as a cautiously optimistic signal that inspector access might soon be restored.
28 Key Person
Maj. Gen. Mohammed Pakpour
/ pækˈpʊər /
Chief commander of the IRGC Ground Forces (and in this context, supervising the naval exercises). A senior figure within Iran's parallel military establishment, Pakpour's public visibility during the Hormuz drills was intended to demonstrate command cohesion and signal that Iran's military leadership was united behind the show of force.
Example sentence
Major General Pakpour's personal supervision of the Hormuz exercises lent them added political weight, suggesting the drills had the direct blessing of Iran's supreme military command.
29 Military
Saber-rattling
/ ˈseɪbər ˌrætəlɪŋ /
The display or threat of military force used as a bargaining tool or political signal, without the immediate intent to actually use that force. It is a form of coercive diplomacy — using the appearance of military readiness to influence an adversary's behaviour at the negotiating table or in the international arena.
Example sentence
Both Washington and Tehran were engaged in textbook saber-rattling — aircraft carriers and IRGC speedboats substituting for the frank communication that neither government could openly conduct.
30 Diplomacy
Islamic Republic
/ ɪzˈlæmɪk rɪˈpʌblɪk /
Iran's formal governmental system since the 1979 revolution, which combines a Shia theocracy with elected republican institutions. The Supreme Leader (currently Ali Khamenei) holds ultimate authority over all state decisions, including foreign policy and nuclear strategy, making him the final arbiter of whether any nuclear deal is acceptable to Iran.
Example sentence
Any deal Araghchi signs in Geneva must ultimately receive the blessing of the Supreme Leader — the Islamic Republic's constitution gives elected officials limited room to negotiate independently on matters of national security.
