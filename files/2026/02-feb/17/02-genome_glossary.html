Nonsense Mutation
Genetics
/ ˈnɒn.sens mjuːˈteɪ.ʃən /
A type of point mutation in DNA where a single nucleotide change creates a premature "stop" signal in the genetic code, causing the cell to stop building a protein too early. The resulting incomplete protein is usually non-functional.
Example sentence
The patient's cystic fibrosis was caused by a nonsense mutation that halted the CFTR protein mid-construction, leaving cells unable to regulate salt and water balance.
02
DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid)
Genetics
/ diː.en.ˈeɪ /
The hereditary molecule found in all living cells, structured as a double helix. It encodes the instructions for building and operating an organism. The sequence of four chemical "letters" (A, T, G, C) spells out the genetic blueprint.
Example sentence
A single altered letter in a person's DNA sequence can disrupt the production of a vital enzyme, triggering a cascade of health problems throughout the body.
03
Premature Stop Codon
Genetics
/ ˈpriːmətʃə stɒp ˈkəʊdɒn /
A three-nucleotide sequence (codon) that signals "end of protein" but appears too early in the gene due to a mutation. The most common disease-causing premature stop codons are TAG, TAA, and TGA. They're like a period inserted mid-sentence.
Example sentence
The newly developed therapy targeted the premature stop codon TAG that was interrupting production of the enzyme needed to prevent lysosomal storage disease.
04
Genome
Genetics
/ ˈdʒiː.nəʊm /
The complete set of genetic material in an organism — all its DNA, including every gene and non-coding sequence. The human genome contains roughly 3 billion base pairs and about 20,000 protein-coding genes, packed into 23 pairs of chromosomes.
Example sentence
Scientists sequenced the patient's entire genome to identify the exact mutation responsible for her rare metabolic disorder.
05
mRNA (Messenger RNA)
Molecule
/ ˈmesɪndʒə ɑː.en.ˈeɪ /
A single-stranded molecule that carries the genetic instructions copied from DNA out of the nucleus to the ribosome. It acts as the working copy of a gene — a temporary blueprint that the cell reads to assemble proteins.
Example sentence
COVID-19 vaccines delivered mRNA instructions into cells, prompting them to manufacture a harmless spike protein and trigger an immune response.
06
tRNA (Transfer RNA)
Molecule
/ trænsˈfɜː ɑː.en.ˈeɪ /
A small RNA molecule that acts as a physical translator in protein synthesis. Each tRNA recognises a specific codon on the mRNA and carries the corresponding amino acid to the ribosome — like a delivery vehicle matching addresses to packages.
Example sentence
The researchers engineered a tRNA to read through the premature stop codon and slot in the correct amino acid, allowing the protein to be completed normally.
07
Suppressor tRNA
Molecule
/ səˈpresər tiː.ɑː.en.ˈeɪ /
A specially engineered or naturally occurring tRNA that "suppresses" a stop codon by reading it as an amino acid instruction instead of a stop signal. It allows ribosomes to continue building a protein past what would normally be a termination point.
Example sentence
By installing a suppressor tRNA into the patient's cells, researchers caused the ribosome to skip over the faulty stop signal and produce a full-length, functional protein.
08
Codon
Molecule
/ ˈkəʊdɒn /
A sequence of three nucleotides in mRNA (or DNA) that specifies a particular amino acid or a stop/start signal. There are 64 possible codons encoding 20 amino acids plus three stop signals. Each codon is the fundamental "word" of the genetic language.
Example sentence
The codon UAG — known as "amber" — is one of three stop signals that normally marks the end of a protein, but when it appears too early, it causes disease.
09
Amino Acid
Molecule
/ əˈmiːnəʊ ˈæsɪd /
The molecular building blocks of proteins. There are 20 standard amino acids, each with a distinct chemical structure. When strung together in specific sequences, they fold into the complex three-dimensional shapes that give proteins their unique functions.
Example sentence
A single substituted amino acid in haemoglobin — glutamic acid swapped for valine — is enough to cause sickle cell disease by altering the protein's shape.
10
pegRNA
Molecule
/ peg ɑː.en.ˈeɪ /
Prime-Editing Guide RNA — a specialised RNA molecule engineered for prime editing. It performs two jobs: first, it guides the editing machinery to the precise location in the genome; second, it carries the corrected DNA template that will be used to rewrite the target sequence.
Example sentence
The team designed a library of 17,000 different pegRNAs to find the best candidate for accessing the tightly packed tRNA gene and installing the suppressor sequence.
11
Ribosome
Biology
/ ˈraɪbəˌsəʊm /
A complex molecular machine found in all cells that reads mRNA instructions and assembles amino acids into proteins in the correct order. Made of RNA and protein, ribosomes are the cell's protein factories — every protein in the body is built by one.
Example sentence
The ribosome stalled when it encountered the premature stop codon, prematurely releasing an incomplete protein that quickly degraded inside the cell.
12
Nucleotide
Biology
/ ˈnjuːklɪətaɪd /
The individual chemical unit (monomer) that makes up DNA and RNA. Each nucleotide consists of a sugar, a phosphate group, and one of four nitrogenous bases. In DNA: adenine (A), thymine (T), guanine (G), cytosine (C). These "letters" are the alphabet of the genetic code.
Example sentence
Changing just one nucleotide — a single letter in three billion — was enough to introduce a stop codon and disrupt the entire gene's function.
13
Transcription
Biology
/ trænsˈkrɪpʃ.ən /
The first step of gene expression — the process by which an enzyme called RNA polymerase copies the DNA sequence of a gene into a complementary mRNA strand. It's analogous to photocopying a master document to send to workers on the factory floor.
Example sentence
During transcription, the cell's machinery reads the mutant gene and faithfully copies the premature stop codon into the mRNA, perpetuating the error.
14
Enzyme
Biology
/ ˈen.zaɪm /
A biological catalyst — a protein that speeds up specific chemical reactions in the body without itself being consumed. Enzymes are essential for virtually every cellular process, from digesting food to copying DNA. A missing or malformed enzyme typically causes serious disease.
Example sentence
After PERT therapy, enzyme activity in Batten disease cell models recovered to 17–70% of normal levels — enough to meaningfully reduce the toxic buildup inside neurons.
15
Homology-Directed Repair (HDR)
Biology
/ həˈmɒlədʒi daɪˈrektɪd rɪˈpeə /
A natural DNA repair mechanism that cells use to fix breaks in DNA using a template — usually the undamaged sister chromosome. In gene therapy, researchers can provide their own template. HDR is highly precise but inefficient, typically achieving only 10–20% success rates in most cell types.
Example sentence
Homology-directed repair was the gold standard for precise gene correction, but PERT's 60–80% efficiency far surpassed it in the same cell models.
16
Prime Editing
Technique
/ praɪm ˈedɪtɪŋ /
An advanced genome-editing technology developed in 2019 that can rewrite specific DNA sequences without making double-strand cuts, reducing errors. It uses a modified CRISPR-Cas9 fused with a reverse transcriptase enzyme guided by a pegRNA. Often described as a "search and replace" for DNA.
Example sentence
Using prime editing, the team permanently rewrote a tRNA gene in human cells with remarkable precision, transforming it into a therapeutic suppressor without off-target effects.
17
PERT
Technique
/ pɜːt /
Prime-Editing-mediated Readthrough of premature Termination codons. The new gene-agnostic therapeutic strategy developed in this study. It permanently edits a redundant tRNA gene in a patient's own genome, turning it into a suppressor tRNA that can read through premature stop signals across many different diseases.
Example sentence
PERT represents a major conceptual shift — rather than correcting each disease gene individually, it reprograms a cell's own machinery to ignore premature stop signals across many genetic disorders.
18
Genome Editing
Technique
/ ˈdʒiːnəʊm ˈedɪtɪŋ /
A group of technologies that allow scientists to add, remove, or alter DNA within a living organism's genome. Methods include CRISPR-Cas9, base editing, and prime editing. It holds the promise of directly curing genetic diseases at their root cause rather than just managing symptoms.
Example sentence
Genome editing tools have progressed so rapidly that what once took years to achieve in research mice can now be performed in human cell cultures within months.
19
AAV9 (Adeno-Associated Virus 9)
Technique
/ eɪ.eɪ.viː naɪn /
A harmless, non-replicating virus used as a gene therapy delivery vehicle (vector). AAV9 is particularly efficient at crossing the blood-brain barrier and reaching the central nervous system, making it favoured for neurological genetic diseases. It can ferry genetic cargo into cells without integrating into the host genome.
Example sentence
The scientists packaged the prime-editing components inside AAV9 and injected it into newborn mice, where it successfully reached the brain, heart, and liver.
20
Base Editing
Technique
/ beɪs ˈedɪtɪŋ /
A genome-editing technique that chemically converts one DNA base letter into another (e.g., C→T or A→G) at a precise location without making double-strand cuts. It is more targeted than traditional CRISPR and is already entering clinical trials for various genetic diseases.
Example sentence
The first clinical use of base editing was reported in an individual with a TAG stop codon mutation, proving that such tools can safely reach and modify disease-causing sites in humans.
21
Batten Disease
Disease
/ ˈbæt.ən dɪˈziːz /
A fatal inherited neurological disorder (also called neuronal ceroid lipofuscinosis) caused by mutations in genes involved in cellular waste disposal. Toxic lipid material accumulates in brain cells, leading to seizures, blindness, progressive cognitive decline, and early death. No cure currently exists.
Example sentence
In Batten disease cell models, installing the engineered suppressor tRNA restored enzyme activity to up to 70% of normal levels — a potentially meaningful threshold for reducing neurodegeneration.
22
Tay-Sachs Disease
Disease
/ ˈteɪ sæks dɪˈziːz /
A rare, inherited lysosomal storage disorder caused by the absence of an enzyme (Hex-A) that breaks down a fatty substance (GM2 ganglioside) in nerve cells. As the substance accumulates, it destroys neurons. The infantile form is invariably fatal, usually before age 5.
Example sentence
Children with Tay-Sachs disease lack functional Hex-A enzyme; PERT therapy demonstrated that restoring even partial enzyme activity might slow the toxic buildup destroying their motor neurons.
23
Niemann-Pick C1 Disease
Disease
/ ˈniːmən pɪk siː wʌn /
A progressive and fatal lysosomal storage disease caused by mutations in the NPC1 gene, disrupting cholesterol and lipid trafficking within cells. Untrafficked cholesterol accumulates in lysosomes, damaging the liver, lungs, and nervous system. Sometimes called "childhood Alzheimer's."
Example sentence
In Niemann-Pick C1 disease cell models treated with PERT, detectable amounts of the normally absent full-length NPC1 protein were produced for the first time.
24
Hurler Syndrome
Disease
/ ˈhɜːlər ˈsɪndrəʊm /
The most severe form of Mucopolysaccharidosis type I (MPS I), a lysosomal storage disorder caused by deficiency of the enzyme alpha-L-iduronidase. Without this enzyme, glycosaminoglycans build up in cells, causing organ damage, skeletal abnormalities, intellectual disability, and shortened lifespan.
Example sentence
In a mouse model of Hurler syndrome, PERT restored 1.7–7% of normal enzyme activity in the brain, heart, and liver — sufficient to improve tissue pathology without triggering toxicity.
25
Cystic Fibrosis
Disease
/ ˈsɪstɪk faɪˈbrəʊsɪs /
A common inherited disease caused by mutations in the CFTR gene, disrupting a chloride channel protein. This causes thick, sticky mucus to build up in the lungs, digestive system, and other organs. It is one of the most prevalent life-limiting genetic disorders, affecting ~100,000 people worldwide.
Example sentence
Some forms of cystic fibrosis stem from nonsense mutations that could, in principle, be targeted by PERT — a single gene-agnostic therapy rather than a mutation-specific one.
