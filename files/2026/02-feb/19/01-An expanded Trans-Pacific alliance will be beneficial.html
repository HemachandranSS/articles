20 terms Geopolitics · Economics · Trade · Diplomacy
01
Rules-based trading system
Noun phrase

An international framework where trade between nations is governed by agreed-upon laws and norms (like WTO rules), rather than by the raw power or whims of dominant countries.

Example

"The rules-based trading system allowed small nations to challenge unfair tariffs imposed by larger powers through the WTO dispute mechanism."

02
Geopolitical rivalries
Noun phrase

Intense competition between nations for power, influence, and strategic advantages — often playing out through trade, military posturing, and diplomatic pressure rather than open war.

Example

"Escalating geopolitical rivalries between the US and China led both countries to restrict technology exports to each other."

03
Middle powers
Noun phrase

Nations that are influential enough to shape international affairs but not dominant enough to set the global agenda alone — such as Canada, Australia, India, Brazil, and South Korea.

Example

"Middle powers like Australia and Canada often punch above their weight by forming coalitions on climate and trade issues."

04
CPTPP
Acronym

The Comprehensive and Progressive Agreement for Trans-Pacific Partnership — a major free trade agreement among 12 countries spanning Asia, Oceania, and the Americas, promoting reduced tariffs and open markets.

Example

"When the UK joined the CPTPP in 2023, it gained preferential access to fast-growing Asian markets for British goods."

05
Weaponisation of interdependence
Noun phrase

The deliberate use of economic ties — trade, finance, technology — as weapons against rival nations. A powerful country exploits another nation's dependence on it to coerce political compliance.

Example

"Cutting off rare-earth mineral exports was a textbook example of the weaponisation of interdependence, pressuring trading partners to change their policies."

06
Supply-chain blackmail
Noun phrase

When a dominant economy threatens to cut off critical goods or inputs (like semiconductors or medicines) from another country's supply chain to force political concessions.

Example

"Nations scrambled to diversify chip production to protect themselves from supply-chain blackmail by major semiconductor exporters."

07
Demographic dividend
Noun phrase

The economic growth potential that arises when a country has a large, young working-age population relative to dependents — more workers producing output, consuming, and paying taxes.

Example

"India is expected to reap a significant demographic dividend over the next two decades as hundreds of millions enter the workforce."

08
Global value chains
Noun phrase

The international network of production stages where different countries contribute different parts of making a product — design in one country, components in another, assembly in a third.

Example

"A modern smartphone passes through over a dozen countries across global value chains before reaching the consumer."

09
Economic coercion
Noun phrase

The use of economic tools — sanctions, tariffs, trade bans, investment restrictions — by one nation to compel another to change its behaviour or policies.

Example

"The sweeping tariffs imposed on agricultural imports were widely condemned as economic coercion designed to bully a smaller trading partner."

10
Digital sovereignty
Noun phrase

A country's or bloc's ability to control its own digital infrastructure, data flows, and technology ecosystem — rather than being dependent on foreign tech giants for critical digital services.

Example

"The EU's push for digital sovereignty includes developing homegrown cloud platforms so European data does not flow through American servers."

11
Grand Bargain
Noun phrase

A major, often difficult political compromise between parties with different interests — each side gives up something significant in exchange for a larger shared benefit.

Example

"The trade deal required a grand bargain: rich nations agreed to lower agricultural subsidies while developing nations accepted stricter IP protections."

12
Carve-outs and exemptions
Noun phrase

Specific exceptions written into trade agreements that allow certain countries to bypass particular rules for defined reasons — often given to developing nations that need policy flexibility to grow.

Example

"India negotiated carve-outs on pharmaceutical patents so it could continue producing affordable generic medicines for its population."

13
Non-Aligned Movement (NAM)
Noun phrase

A Cold War-era grouping of countries — led by India, Egypt, and Yugoslavia — that refused to formally align with either the US-led Western bloc or the Soviet bloc, charting an independent foreign policy.

Example

"India's founding role in the Non-Aligned Movement gave it moral authority as a voice for post-colonial nations seeking independence from superpower domination."

14
Force multiplier
Noun phrase

Originally a military term, it refers to any factor that dramatically increases the effectiveness or impact of a group or strategy — something that makes the whole far greater than the sum of its parts.

Example

"India's massive consumer market acts as a force multiplier for the trade bloc, making membership far more attractive for technology exporters."

15
Bretton Woods organisations
Noun phrase

International financial institutions created at the 1944 Bretton Woods Conference, primarily the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the World Bank, designed to manage the global financial system and fund development.

Example

"Developing nations have long argued that the Bretton Woods organisations were designed to serve Western interests and need fundamental reform."

16
Intellectual property (IP)
Noun phrase

Legal rights — patents, copyrights, trademarks — that protect creations of the mind (inventions, software, brands, designs). A frequent flashpoint in trade negotiations, as strict IP rules can restrict access for developing countries.

Example

"Negotiations stalled over intellectual property protections, as wealthy nations demanded stricter enforcement while developing countries feared it would block tech transfer."

17
Counterweight
Noun

A force, power, or alliance that offsets or balances the dominance of another — preventing any single entity from becoming overwhelmingly powerful in a system.

Example

"A unified ASEAN was seen as a critical counterweight to China's growing economic and military influence in Southeast Asia."

18
Developmental imperatives
Noun phrase

The unique, pressing economic and social needs of developing countries — like building infrastructure, reducing poverty, and nurturing infant industries — that may require different policies than those suitable for advanced economies.

Example

"Trade deals must respect the developmental imperatives of poorer nations, allowing them to subsidise key sectors until they are globally competitive."

19
Trans-regional alliance
Noun phrase

A partnership or coalition that spans multiple geographic regions of the world — going beyond a single continent or neighbourhood to connect countries across vast distances through shared interests.

Example

"The proposed trans-regional alliance linking Asia, Europe, and Latin America would be unprecedented in both its geographic scope and economic weight."

20
WTO (World Trade Organization)
Noun / Acronym

The international body that regulates trade between nations, provides a forum for negotiating trade agreements, and settles trade disputes according to agreed rules. Seen as increasingly paralysed by great-power conflicts.

Example

"Several countries filed a case at the WTO, arguing that the new tariffs violated the most-favoured-nation principle enshrined in global trade rules."
