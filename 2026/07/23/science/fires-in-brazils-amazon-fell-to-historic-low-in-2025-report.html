Fires in Brazil’s Amazon fell to historic low in 2025: report

Protecting the Amazon is crucial to combat climate change. Fewer fires, the report said, 'interrupts a sequence of years of fire expansion and returns the country to a historically lower level of burned area'

AGENCE FRANCE-PRESSE

Environment & Ecology  |  Biodiversity & Global Climate Governance

Forest fires in the Brazilian Amazon fell to a historic low in 2025 after peaking the previous year, according to a report published on Tuesday.

The data gathered by environmental monitoring network MapBiomas show that fires eased off across the country as well, with burned areas nationwide at their smallest since 2018.

Flames engulfed the world’s largest tropical rainforest in 2024 when an unprecedented drought triggered the spread of fires, the majority of which were man-made.

Using satellite images, MapBiomas found that last year saw a reversal of the critical situation, with 3.1 million hectares burned – the smallest area since records began in 1985.

The decline was “out of the ordinary,” geographer and report coordinator Ane Alencar told presspersons.

A delayed rainy season between 2024 and 2025 blocked the emergence of the “ideal window for burning in certain areas” where fire is used to clear agricultural land, Ms. Alencar said.

Poorly managed fires of this type are exacerbated by illicit practices such as deforestation, which is common in areas where illegal mining occurs.

Deforestation also eased off last year, MapBiomas said, which drastically reduces the amount of fuel available to start fires.

The report highlighted the “psychological factor” among communities who feared a fire season like 2024 and therefore helped control any outbreaks.

Fewer fires, it said, “interrupts a sequence of years of fire expansion and returns the country to a historically lower level of burned area.”

Central Brazil’s vast Cerrado savanna, however, “kept burning” at similar rates, Ms. Alencar said.

She also flagged the potential arrival of a “super El Nino” this year, referring to a climate phenomenon that brings “an extended dry season and a greater risk of forest fires.”

Protecting the Amazon is crucial to combat climate change, since its dense vegetation absorbs the greenhouse gases that cause global warming.

Official data from the first quarter of 2025 shows that deforestation is at its lowest-ever rate this decade, with President Luiz Inacio Lula da Silva pledging to eradicate the practice by 2030.

The latest report will come as good news for the leftist President ahead of seeking reelection in October where he will be looking to nab a fourth non-consecutive term in office, partly by championing environmental causes.

But his decision to endorse a major offshore oil exploration project off the coast of the Amazon has garnered virulent criticism from activists.
