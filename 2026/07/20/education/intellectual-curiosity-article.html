A bright, hardworking child genuinely good at academics and with a stellar profile is stumped when asked: "What are you curious about? What do you find yourself thinking about when nobody's asking you to think about anything?" They're not sure and have no answer; surprisingly, they've never really thought about this before. But when researching what the Ivy League and top universities look for these days, they will all say, "intellectual curiosity…"

Third Pillar

So, what is "intellectual curiosity?" In 2011, a team of psychologists at the University of Edinburgh, the U.K., studied data from around 200 studies involving 50,000 students and asked: what actually predicts how well a student does academically? The predictable answers were intelligence, conscientiousness, working hard, showing up, and being disciplined. Then came the finding that should have changed how we think about education: Intellectual curiosity, the hunger to explore, to understand, to keep asking why... This impacts academic performance almost as much as intelligence itself. The researchers called it "the third pillar". Intelligence, effort, curiosity. All three matter. Yet our education system spends almost all of its energy on the first two and almost none on the third.

In 2022, PISA, the international assessment that tests 15-year-olds across 81 countries, found that curiosity had the greatest relationship with performance in Maths compared to any other factor measured. Eighty-one points separated students with high curiosity from students with low curiosity. That is not a small gap. The same research also consistently finds that curiosity declines as children get older, not because they become less curious but because systems around them teach them that curiosity is not being rewarded.

Why does this matter now? Think about what a typical high-achieving Indian classroom actually rewards: recall, speed, accuracy on a defined set of problems covering the chapter, answering in the exact words used in the textbook, and finishing the paper on time. Now think about what curiosity requires. Time to wander. Permission to ask a question with no guaranteed answer. The safety of not knowing something yet and finding that interesting rather than threatening.

Research has found that when children enter school, the number of questions they ask per hour actually declines and stays down throughout primary school. Not because they have fewer questions. But because they learn quickly that asking questions is not what school is for. When feedback is about rankings and scores, curiosity goes down. When feedback is about whether you understood something correctly, curiosity goes up.

Crucial Skills

The World Economic Forum surveys over 1,000 of the world's largest employers every year and asks them what skills they need most. In the 2025 report covering 14 million workers across 55 countries, curiosity and lifelong learning appeared in the top 10 core skills. Analytical thinking was number one. Creative thinking was number four. All three require the same underlying disposition: a person who finds problems interesting, not threatening; someone who can sit with uncertainty and not get overwhelmed; someone who actually wants to understand rather than produce the right output.

Intellectual curiosity is not a technical skill. You cannot teach it with a coding bootcamp. It is built or destroyed over years of schooling, by the questions a teacher encourages or discourages, by whether a child feels safe not knowing yet, by what gets celebrated in a classroom or, for that matter, even at home.

Intellectual curiosity is not a technical skill. It is built or destroyed over years of schooling — by the questions a teacher encourages or discourages.

The NEP 2020 encourages inquiry-based, competency-led education and calls for a massive shift away from rote learning. But policy and practical implementation are completely different things. The CBSE allows any combination of subjects but schools face curricular challenges, board exams and parents who want marks.

But curiosity doesn't require a curriculum overhaul. It requires small, deliberate choices. It's noticing which students are quietly curious in ways that don't show up in marks and finding a way to feed that, rather than smooth it over. Today, machines are getting better every year. But what we're not encouraging is what these machines actually need: asking better questions to get better answers!
